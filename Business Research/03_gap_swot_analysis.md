## Gap Analysis

### What Problems Remain Unsolved Today?

| Gap | Current State | Impact | Our Solution |
|-----|--------------|--------|-------------|
| **No pre-deployment TCO estimation** | Teams guess costs or use biased vendor calculators | $500K-$5M in cost surprises annually | Dynamic pricing engine with 63+ component cost models |
| **No compatibility validation before build** | Discovered during integration testing (weeks/months in) | 3-6 month project delays | Real-time compatibility matrix with fix recommendations |
| **No objective scoring framework** | Decisions based on gut feel, vendor demos, or Gartner quadrant position | Confirmation bias, political decisions | 9 weighted KPI scoring with explainability |
| **No stack-level optimization** | Components evaluated in isolation | Suboptimal combinations, integration debt | Combination scoring with bonus/penalty system |
| **No M&A consolidation intelligence** | Consultants ($500K+) manually map landscapes | 70-90% of M&A value destroyed by poor IT integration | Automated stack overlap detection and consolidation recommendations |
| **No architecture "what-if" simulation** | Build → Discover problems → Rebuild | Massive waste of engineering cycles | Architecture Digital Twin with scenario modeling |
| **No vendor lock-in quantification** | Qualitative "high/medium/low" assessments | Underestimated switching costs | Quantified lock-in score with migration difficulty ratings |

## SWOT Analysis

### Strengths
- **First-mover in category intersection** — No competitor combines visual builder + scoring + compatibility + TCO
- **Deep technology knowledge graph** — 63+ technology profiles with 9 KPIs, 30+ attributes each
- **Explainability as a core principle** — Every score decomposes into evidence, not black boxes
- **Data-stack-native** — Purpose-built for data architecture, not generic EA
- **Already built** — Working prototype with scoring engine, compatibility rules, and visual canvas

### Weaknesses
- **Cold-start data problem** — Technology profiles need continuous updating
- **Single-developer risk** — No team redundancy yet
- **No customer validation** — All evidence is market research, not customer interviews
- **Limited brand recognition** — Competing against SAP (LeanIX), Microsoft, established players
- **Scoring subjectivity** — KPI scores assigned by builders, not independent benchmarks (yet)

### Opportunities
- **Category creation** — "Data Architecture Decision Intelligence" doesn't exist as a category
- **AI integration moat** — Knowledge graph + scoring rules = structured data for AI agents
- **Community-driven benchmarks** — Crowdsource real-world performance data to build data moat
- **Consulting firm partnerships** — White-label scoring engine for advisory firms
- **M&A advisory niche** — High-value, event-driven use case with clear ROI
- **Compliance-driven demand** — RBI/GDPR creating urgent need for architecture documentation

### Threats
- **Platform incumbents expanding** — LeanIX, Ardoq could add data-stack-specific scoring
- **AI copilots commoditizing advice** — ChatGPT/Claude can give architecture recommendations (without scoring)
- **Vendor-sponsored tools** — Snowflake, Databricks, Confluent building their own architecture advisors
- **Enterprise sales cycle** — Long procurement timelines for new categories
- **Free alternatives** — Draw.io + ChatGPT might be "good enough" for some teams

## Porter's Five Forces

| Force | Intensity | Analysis |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Threat of New Entrants** | Medium | Low capital barriers but high knowledge barriers. Building 63+ accurate tech profiles takes significant domain expertise. |
| **Bargaining Power of Buyers** | Medium-High | Enterprise buyers have many alternatives for individual capabilities. Need to demonstrate unique combined value. |
| **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** | Low | Open-source technology data, public vendor documentation. No supplier dependency. |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | Medium | Excel + ChatGPT + Draw.io is a "good enough" substitute for small teams. Enterprise teams need more rigor. |
| **Competitive Rivalry** | Low (in exact category) | No direct competitor does what we do. Adjacent competitors are strong but differently focused. |
